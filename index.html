More About Ocean Gardens

Our planet, when viewed from space, appears as a water world with oceans covering over 70% of the Earth's surface and holding about 97% of the planet's surface water. Life thrives in these vast oceanic environments, from the surface to the extreme depths of the deepest underwater trenches. In fact, more than 99% of Earth's habitable space exists within our oceans, making our planet truly an "ocean planet." NASA has been observing Earth's oceans from space for over two decades. The agency's first civilian oceanographic satellite, Seasat, was launched on June 28, 1978. This pioneering satellite carried five specialized sensors designed to monitor various aspects of the oceans from space.

  • - A radar altimeter to measure spacecraft height above the ocean surface
  • - A microwave scatterometer to measure wind speed and direction
  • - A scanning multichannel microwave radiometer to measure sea surface temperature
  • - A visible and infrared radiometer to identify cloud, land and water features
  • - A synthetic aperture radar to monitor the global surface wave field and polar sea ice conditions

Marine Ecosystems

Marine ecosystems are vital for human life in several ways: Food: They provide over half of the world's seafood, a critical source of protein and nutrients. Oxygen: Marine ecosystems, especially phytoplankton, produce about half of Earth's oxygen. Climate Regulation: Oceans help regulate the planet's climate by absorbing carbon dioxide and influencing temperature and precipitation patterns. Coastal Protection: Coastal ecosystems like mangroves and coral reefs act as natural barriers against erosion and storms, safeguarding coastal communities. Recreation and Tourism: Activities such as swimming, boating, and fishing generate tourism revenue, boosting local economies. Economic Benefits: Industries like fisheries, aquaculture, tourism, and shipping rely on marine resources, creating jobs and economic opportunities.

ocean-provided services

NASA plays a crucial role in studying and monitoring the ocean, helping us understand its vital services for human life:

1. Climate Control: Oceans store heat, regulate climate, and influence the water cycle.

2. Food Source: Oceans supply seafood, feeding billions through fisheries and aquaculture.

3. Biodiversity: Diverse ocean life benefits us with new medicines and ecosystem health. 4. Recreation and Tourism: The ocean offers activities like swimming, boating, fishing, and surfing. NASA research helps understand and protect these ocean services. It monitors sea level rise, manages fisheries, and safeguards marine ecosystems, ensuring the well-being of coastal communities and future generations.

Marine Phytoplankton

Marine phytoplankton are tiny, sunlight-driven ocean organisms that: - Serve as food for marine life and humans. - Produce oxygen and absorb carbon dioxide. - Have various uses, like in fish food and cosmetics. To protect phytoplankton: - Reduce pollution. - Preserve marine habitats. - Manage fishing sustainably. - Support research. They're vital for ocean and Earth health, and safeguarding them is essential.
